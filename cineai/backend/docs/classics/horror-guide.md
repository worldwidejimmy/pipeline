# Horror Cinema — The Essential Guide

## What Horror Does

Horror is the only genre whose primary function is to produce a physiological response — fear. But the best horror films are not merely frightening. They are about something: the body and what it can do (Cronenberg), family and what it hides (Hereditary, The Shining), the monsters society creates (Get Out, Candyman), sexual violence and its aftermath (A Nightmare on Elm Street, Rosemary's Baby), grief and its distortions (Midsommar, The Babadook).

Horror is also the genre most willing to kill its protagonists. The breaking of the audience's safety contract — anything can happen — is where its power lives.

## The Canon

### Psycho (1960) — dir. Alfred Hitchcock ★★★★★
Hitchcock killed his protagonist in the first act and cinema was never the same. The shower scene is so technically accomplished (71 cuts in 45 seconds) that it has been analyzed more than almost any scene in film history. Anthony Perkins' Norman Bates is the prototype for every sympathetic movie monster that followed. The film plays on the audience's complicity — we identify with Norman before we understand what he is. Bernard Herrmann's strings score is inseparable from our understanding of cinematic dread.

### Rosemary's Baby (1968) — dir. Roman Polanski ★★★★★
A young woman (Mia Farrow) is pregnant and begins to suspect her neighbors have occult designs on her child. Polanski never shows the monster — the horror is in the gaslighting, in Rosemary's growing certainty that she is right and the world's certainty that she is unwell. The film is about how society — husbands, doctors, neighbors — pathologizes women's bodies and knowledge. Still terrifying 55 years later.

### The Exorcist (1973) — dir. William Friedkin ★★★★★
Twelve-year-old Regan MacNeil (Linda Blair) is possessed by a demon. William Peter Blatty adapted his own novel. Friedkin shot in practical locations (the Georgetown house) and used practical effects (the head rotation was a puppet; the levitation used wire) to create images of bodily violation that remain disturbing. The film is genuinely about faith and doubt — the older priest, Father Merrin, has spent his life fighting this entity. It's his exhaustion and belief that makes the ending matter. The highest-grossing horror film until It (2017).

### The Shining (1980) — dir. Stanley Kubrick ★★★★★
Jack Torrance (Jack Nicholson), his wife Wendy (Shelley Duvall), and their son Danny winter in the Overlook Hotel as its caretakers. Kubrick adapted Stephen King's novel and King famously hated the adaptation. What King wrote as a ghost story about addiction and family, Kubrick transformed into something more abstract: the hotel as a machine that amplifies what's already there. Jack Nicholson plays a man already unraveling. The Steadicam shots through the Overlook's corridors are cinema's most unsettling use of space. "Here's Johnny" was improvised.

### Alien (1979) — dir. Ridley Scott ★★★★★
See: Sci-Fi Cinema Guide. Also the greatest horror film set in space. The chest burst scene caused audience members to vomit and faint at the premiere. H.R. Giger's design for the xenomorph — simultaneously insectoid, mechanical, and phallic — remains the most disturbing creature in film history.

### The Thing (1982) — dir. John Carpenter ★★★★★
A research team in Antarctica discovers an alien life form that can perfectly imitate any organism. John Carpenter's masterpiece of paranoia — if anyone could be the Thing, trust becomes impossible. Rob Bottin's practical effects (pre-CGI body horror: torsos opening, heads detaching on spider legs) are still the most viscerally effective creature work in cinema. Kurt Russell as the pragmatic survivor. The ending is one of cinema's great refusals of resolution.

### A Nightmare on Elm Street (1984) — dir. Wes Craven ★★★★
Freddy Krueger (Robert Englund) kills teenagers in their dreams. Craven's insight: dreams are the one place you cannot be safe, because you cannot choose not to sleep. The concept is genuinely terrifying and the original film honors it — Freddy is frightening, not the quip-machine he becomes in the sequels. Johnny Depp's film debut.

### Misery (1990) — dir. Rob Reiner ★★★★
A novelist (James Caan) crashes his car near the home of his "number one fan" (Kathy Bates). Psychological horror without a monster — the monster is love taken to its logical extreme. Bates won the Oscar; her performance, swinging between maternal warmth and annihilating rage, is one of the great horror performances. The hobbling scene.

### Scream (1996) — dir. Wes Craven ★★★★
The film that saved the slasher genre by being smarter than the genre. Neve Campbell and Courteney Cox. The characters in Scream know the rules of horror movies and discuss them while they're being murdered according to those rules. Kevin Williamson's script is both a horror movie and a critique of horror movies simultaneously. The opening sequence with Drew Barrymore is one of the genre's finest first acts.

### The Sixth Sense (1999) — dir. M. Night Shyamalan ★★★★
A child psychologist (Bruce Willis) treats a boy who sees dead people. The twist is one of cinema's most successfully engineered surprises — and the film is constructed so that every scene recontextualizes on a second viewing. After the twist, the film you thought you were watching becomes a tragedy.

### The Ring (2002) — dir. Gore Verbinski ★★★★
An adaptation of the Japanese Ringu. A journalist investigates a videotape that kills everyone who watches it within seven days. Gore Verbinski's direction (desaturated color, the Pacific Northwest in perpetual grey) is perfect for the material. The image of Samara climbing from the television remains one of the most effective jump scares because it is held, and held, past the point of jump-scare convention.

### The Descent (2005) — dir. Neil Marshall ★★★★★
Six women go cave-diving in North Carolina. The first forty minutes are the most effective claustrophobia in cinema — before anything supernatural happens, the caves are horrifying. What follows (creatures, the group fracturing, the revelation of what the caves contain in human terms) is the finest British horror film of the century.

### Hereditary (2018) — dir. Ari Aster ★★★★★
A family unravels after the death of a secretive grandmother. Toni Collette's performance is the finest in a horror film in decades. Ari Aster's debut is about grief as possession — the way family trauma inhabits and replicates itself across generations. The film contains the single most shocking sudden death in horror history. The final act is maximally disturbing. Not for casual viewing.

### Get Out (2017) — dir. Jordan Peele ★★★★★
A Black man (Daniel Kaluuya) visits his white girlfriend's family estate and begins to notice something deeply wrong. Jordan Peele's debut film uses horror conventions to explore the experience of being Black in white liberal America — the disorienting performance of non-racism, the fetishization, the extraction. One of the most politically urgent films of the 21st century. The "sunken place" is one of cinema's great metaphors.

### Midsommar (2019) — dir. Ari Aster ★★★★
A grieving young woman (Florence Pugh) joins her boyfriend's anthropology research trip to a Swedish midsummer festival. The horror happens entirely in daylight. Aster's film about a relationship that should have ended and a community that provides what it didn't — belonging, validation, ritual — is the most emotionally complex horror film of recent years. The extended cut is better.

## Horror Subgenres

**Slasher:** Halloween, Friday the 13th, Scream, A Nightmare on Elm Street
**Supernatural/possession:** The Exorcist, Hereditary, Sinister, The Conjuring
**Psychological:** The Shining, Repulsion, Black Swan, Rosemary's Baby
**Body horror:** The Thing, The Fly, Annihilation, Videodrome
**Elevated/arthouse:** Hereditary, Midsommar, Get Out, The Witch, It Follows
**Found footage:** The Blair Witch Project, Cloverfield, Paranormal Activity
**Creature feature:** Alien, Jaws, Predator, The Descent
**J-Horror:** The Ring, The Grudge, Audition, Pulse
