# Comedy Cinema — Genre Guide

## What Makes Great Screen Comedy
Comedy is the hardest genre to get right and the one most likely to age badly. The best screen comedies survive because they're built on something true about human behaviour — embarrassment, pretension, desire, status anxiety — rather than just jokes. Timing is everything: in dialogue (the pause before the punchline), in editing (the cut away from the reaction), and in performance (the glance that sells what the line sets up).

## The Silent Era Foundation
Buster Keaton and Charlie Chaplin established the two poles of film comedy that still define the genre:
- **Buster Keaton** — stone-faced, physically extraordinary, films as elegant machines. The General (1926), Sherlock Jr. (1924). Keaton performed his own stunts at genuine risk; the stunts are the comedy.
- **Charlie Chaplin** — emotional, sentimental, politically engaged. Modern Times (1936), The Great Dictator (1940), City Lights (1931). The Little Tramp is cinema's first enduring character.

## Screwball Comedy (1930s–40s)
Fast-talking, battle-of-the-sexes comedies produced during the studio era:
- **It Happened One Night (1934)** — Rating: 8.2. Clark Gable and Claudette Colbert. Won all five major Oscars. The template for romantic comedies.
- **Bringing Up Baby (1938)** — Rating: 7.8. Cary Grant and Katharine Hepburn. A paleontologist, a socialite, and a leopard.
- **His Girl Friday (1940)** — Rating: 7.9. The fastest talking film ever made. Journalism, crime, and divorce.

## British Comedy
British comedy has a distinct sensibility: class-consciousness, understatement, embarrassment as the primary comic engine:
- **Kind Hearts and Coronets (1949)** — Rating: 8.1. Alec Guinness plays all eight members of a noble family being murdered by their low-born relative.
- **The Lavender Hill Mob (1951)** — Rating: 7.5. Alec Guinness again. A mild bank clerk plans an audacious gold bullion heist.
- **Dr. Strangelove (1964)** — Rating: 8.4. Kubrick's nuclear satire. Peter Sellers in three roles.
- **Monty Python and the Holy Grail (1975)** — Rating: 8.2. Anarchic medieval satire. The Black Knight. The Knights Who Say Ni. "It's only a flesh wound."

## American Comedy: 1970s–90s
- **Annie Hall (1977)** — Rating: 8.0. Woody Allen. The romantic comedy as deconstruction. Characters speak to camera; scenes happen out of order; the relationship ends before the film begins.
- **This Is Spinal Tap (1984)** — Rating: 8.0. Rob Reiner's mockumentary about a failing British heavy metal band. Invented the mockumentary as a format.
- **Ferris Bueller's Day Off (1986)** — Rating: 7.8. John Hughes. A perfect day for a teenager in Chicago. Matthew Broderick at peak charisma.
- **When Harry Met Sally (1989)** — Rating: 7.6. Nora Ephron. The definitive romantic comedy. The diner scene. "I'll have what she's having."
- **Groundhog Day (1993)** — Rating: 8.0. Bill Murray relives the same day in Punxsutawney, Pennsylvania. What begins as a comedy becomes a meditation on growth and love.
- **The Big Lebowski (1998)** — Rating: 8.1. The Coen Brothers. Jeff Bridges as "The Dude." A mistaken-identity caper that becomes a shaggy-dog meditation on Los Angeles and masculinity.

## Comedy in the 2000s–2010s
- **Superbad (2007)** — Rating: 7.6. Judd Apatow production. High school seniors trying to get alcohol. Genuinely sweet underneath the raunchiness.
- **The Grand Budapest Hotel (2014)** — Rating: 8.1. Wes Anderson. A legendary hotel concierge and his lobby boy embroiled in murder and theft. Anderson at his most ornate and most funny.
- **What We Do in the Shadows (2014)** — Rating: 7.7. Taika Waititi and Jemaine Clement. A mockumentary about vampire flatmates in New Zealand. Led to a hugely successful TV spin-off.
- **Get Out (2017)** — Rating: 7.7. Jordan Peele. A Black man visits his white girlfriend's family. Starts as social satire, becomes horror. Demonstrates that comedy and horror share exactly the same underlying mechanism.

## Stand-Up Comedy Translated to Film
- **Annie Hall** is partly a stand-up routine in narrative form
- **Funny People (2009)** — Judd Apatow's most personal film, about a dying comedian
- **Bo Burnham: Inside (2021)** — A pandemic-era one-man show that became one of the most discussed films of its year

## For a Great Comedy Night
**Classics:** Dr. Strangelove, Groundhog Day, The Big Lebowski, Annie Hall
**Modern:** The Grand Budapest Hotel, What We Do in the Shadows, Get Out
**Pure fun:** Monty Python and the Holy Grail, Ferris Bueller's Day Off, Superbad
