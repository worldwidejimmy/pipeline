# The 1980s — Blockbusters, Indies, and the MTV Era

## The Decade Defined

The 1980s was the decade of the blockbuster's consolidation and the simultaneous rise of American independent cinema. On one side: Spielberg, Lucas, and their imitators filling multiplexes with high-concept entertainment. On the other: John Cassavetes, Jim Jarmusch, Spike Lee, and the Sundance generation making personal films outside the studio system. Both traditions flourished simultaneously, and the tension between them defined American cinema for the next four decades.

## The Essential Films

### Raging Bull (1980) — dir. Martin Scorsese ★★★★★
Robert De Niro gained 60 pounds to play the aging Jake LaMotta, shot in black and white by Michael Chapman. The boxing sequences are among the most kinetic ever filmed — Scorsese and editor Thelma Schoonmaker cut to the rhythm of violence rather than the rhythm of sport. But Raging Bull is not a boxing film; it is a study of self-destruction, jealousy, and masculine inadequacy. Joe Pesci won the Oscar for Tommy DeVito. Consistently ranked one of the greatest films ever made.

### E.T. the Extra-Terrestrial (1982) — dir. Steven Spielberg ★★★★★
A 10-year-old boy in suburban California befriends an alien left behind by his spacecraft. Spielberg shot largely from child-height perspective, creating a world seen from below. John Williams' score. The bicycle silhouette against the moon. The film's emotional intelligence — its treatment of loneliness, friendship, and loss — far exceeds its reputation as family entertainment. The highest-grossing film of all time at its release.

### Blade Runner (1982) — dir. Ridley Scott ★★★★★
Los Angeles, 2019. Replicant hunter Rick Deckard (Harrison Ford) pursues four escaped synthetic humans. Scott's production design — influenced by Moebius, H.R. Giger, and Fritz Lang's Metropolis — created the definitive visual template for science fiction. Rutger Hauer's "tears in rain" speech was largely improvised. The film was a box office disappointment in 1982; its reputation grew through home video and the 1992 Director's Cut. Ridley Scott's suggestion that Deckard himself may be a replicant has never been definitively resolved.

### Do the Right Thing (1989) — dir. Spike Lee ★★★★★
The hottest day of the year in Bedford-Stuyvesant, Brooklyn. Racial tensions escalate until violence. Lee's film refused to offer easy answers — the final titles quote both Martin Luther King Jr. and Malcolm X on violence — and the Academy's failure to nominate it for Best Picture remains one of the great Oscar scandals. Ernest Dickerson's cinematography: oversaturated colors, low angles, heat. The Radio Raheem "love and hate" speech. Mookie throwing the trash can.

### Blue Velvet (1986) — dir. David Lynch ★★★★★
Jeffrey Beaumont (Kyle MacLachlan) finds a severed human ear in a field in small-town America and descends into a criminal underworld. Lynch's vision of American normalcy as a surface beneath which violence, sexual perversion, and psychosis permanently boil. Dennis Hopper as Frank Booth — one of cinema's most disturbing villains. Isabella Rossellini. The film is about the seduction of the dark, and it implicates the viewer in Jeffrey's voyeurism.

### Back to the Future (1985) — dir. Robert Zemeckis ★★★★
Teenager Marty McFly (Michael J. Fox) is accidentally sent back to 1955 in a time machine built by eccentric scientist Doc Brown (Christopher Lloyd). The screenplay by Zemeckis and Bob Gale is a model of comic construction — every setup pays off, every payoff was set up. The film is also, quietly, a meditation on how the choices of parents shape their children.

### The Shining (1980) — dir. Stanley Kubrick ★★★★★
See also: Horror Guide. Jack Torrance (Jack Nicholson) takes a job as off-season caretaker at the Overlook Hotel. Kubrick's adaptation of Stephen King's novel transformed it from a ghost story about addiction into something more abstract and disturbing. The Steadicam corridors. Room 237. "Here's Johnny." Shelley Duvall's performance, achieved under conditions of deliberate psychological stress by Kubrick, is one of the decade's most underrated.

### Platoon (1986) — dir. Oliver Stone ★★★★
The Vietnam War from the perspective of a foot soldier (Charlie Sheen) caught between a humane sergeant (Tom Berenger) and a murderous one (Willem Dafoe). Stone served in Vietnam; the film has the authority of experience. Won Best Picture.

### Full Metal Jacket (1987) — dir. Stanley Kubrick ★★★★
Vietnam in two halves: the dehumanizing boot camp at Parris Island (R. Lee Ermey's Gunnery Sergeant Hartman is one of cinema's great performances), and the surreal combat in Hue. The two halves feel deliberately disjointed — the point is that the training creates something that war then deploys.

### Amadeus (1984) — dir. Miloš Forman ★★★★★
The rivalry between Antonio Salieri (F. Murray Abraham) and Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart (Tom Hulce) as a meditation on mediocrity, genius, and the cruelty of God. Abraham's Salieri — the patron saint of mediocrity — is one of cinema's great performances. Won eight Academy Awards including Best Picture.

## John Hughes and Teen Cinema

John Hughes defined 1980s American teen cinema with films of unexpected emotional intelligence: Sixteen Candles (1984), The Breakfast Club (1985), Ferris Bueller's Day Off (1986), Pretty in Pink (1986), Some Kind of Wonderful (1987). Hughes understood adolescent experience with a specificity no previous filmmaker had achieved, and his soundtracks — Psychedelic Furs, Simple Minds, OMD, New Order — made his films inseparable from the era's music.

## The Rise of Independents

Jim Jarmusch's Stranger Than Paradise (1984) — shot for $125,000 — proved American independent cinema could find international distribution and critical success. Blood Simple (1984) launched the Coen Brothers. Spike Lee's She's Gotta Have It (1986) launched a new voice in Black American cinema. The decade ended with sex, lies, and videotape (1989) winning the Palme d'Or and establishing Sundance as the primary venue for American independent cinema.
