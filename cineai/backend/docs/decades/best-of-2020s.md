# The 2020s — Cinema After the Pandemic

## The Decade So Far

The pandemic closed cinemas globally in 2020 and forced a restructuring of how films are released, watched, and valued. Streaming became the default delivery mechanism for most films. The theatrical experience survived — but as a premium event for spectacle rather than a default — and the films that thrived theatrically were either blockbusters or art cinema events. The decade is still young, but several films have already established themselves as significant.

---

## The Essential Films (2020-2024)

### The Power of the Dog (2021) — dir. Jane Campion ★★★★★
Montana, 1925. Rancher Phil Burbank (Benedict Cumberbatch) torments his brother's new wife (Kirsten Dunst) and finds himself drawn to her son (Kodi Smit-McPhee). Campion's adaptation of Thomas Savage's novel is a Western unlike any other — its violence is psychological, its revelations retroactive, its final act a complete reframing of everything that came before. Cumberbatch's performance is the finest of his career. Won Best Director.

### Tár (2022) — dir. Todd Field ★★★★★
Lydia Tár (Cate Blanchett), world-renowned conductor, begins to unravel as accusations of abuse surface. Field's film — the director's first in sixteen years — is a three-hour character study that refuses moral simplicity. Blanchett's performance is the decade's finest so far, a complete inhabitation of a towering, flawed intellect. The film is also a meditation on power, art, and whether a monster can make beauty.

### Everything Everywhere All at Once (2022) — dir. Daniel Kwan & Daniel Scheinert ★★★★★
A Chinese-American laundromat owner discovers she can access the skills and memories of her parallel-universe selves. The Daniels' film is maximalist, chaotic, and overwhelming — and somehow emotionally devastating. Michelle Yeoh. Ke Huy Quan's return to acting. Jamie Lee Curtis. The film won seven Academy Awards including Best Picture, Best Director, and all three acting categories except Best Actor.

### Oppenheimer (2023) — dir. Christopher Nolan ★★★★★
The life of J. Robert Oppenheimer (Cillian Murphy), the theoretical physicist who led the Manhattan Project. Nolan's three-hour film is structured around two parallel hearings — Oppenheimer's 1954 security clearance hearing in color, and Lewis Strauss's 1959 Senate confirmation hearing in black and white — and the intercutting gradually reveals each as a frame that distorts the truth. The Trinity test sequence, filmed practically with no CGI. Emily Blunt, Robert Downey Jr., Matt Damon. Won seven Academy Awards.

### Past Lives (2023) — dir. Celine Song ★★★★★
Two childhood friends from Seoul — Nora (Greta Lee) and Hae Sung (Teo Yoo) — reconnect twice as adults, separated by an ocean and two decades of different lives. Song's debut feature is quietly devastating: a film about the lives not lived, the selves left behind in other countries, other languages, other choices. The bar scene at the end is one of the most emotionally precise scenes in recent cinema.

### The Banshees of Inisherin (2022) — dir. Martin McDonagh ★★★★★
On a small Irish island in 1923 — against the backdrop of the Civil War on the mainland — Colm (Brendan Gleeson) decides he no longer wants to be friends with Pádraic (Colin Farrell), and Pádraic cannot accept it. McDonagh's film is simultaneously a dark comedy, a meditation on art versus companionship, and an allegory for civil war. Barry Keoghan. The escalating consequences of stubbornness. Gleeson's self-mutilation as artistic statement. Both leads won at various awards ceremonies.

### Aftersun (2022) — dir. Charlotte Wells ★★★★★
A woman in her early thirties re-watches home video footage from a holiday in Turkey she took with her father when she was eleven. Wells' debut film — working from personal memory — is structured around what the camera didn't capture: what the adult Sophie now understands about her father's mental state that the child couldn't. Paul Mescal's performance, entirely interior, is the decade's most heartbreaking.

### Killers of the Flower Moon (2023) — dir. Martin Scorsese ★★★★
The systematic murder of Osage Nation members in 1920s Oklahoma for their oil rights. Scorsese's three-and-a-half-hour film — adapted from David Grann's book — is structured around complicity: Ernest Burkhart (Leonardo DiCaprio) participates in the murders of his own wife's family under the direction of his uncle William Hale (Robert De Niro). Lily Gladstone as Mollie Burkhart. A film about American horror that doesn't allow its white protagonists to be redeemed.

## The Blockbuster Landscape

**Top Gun: Maverick (2022)** proved the theatrical blockbuster could still draw mass audiences — $1.49 billion worldwide. **Avatar: The Way of Water (2022)** — $2.3 billion, proving spectacle still sells. **Barbie (2023)** — $1.44 billion, proving concept-driven original IP could compete with franchise sequels.

---

## What the Decade Is About

The early 2020s films repeatedly return to identity, memory, and grief — characters assembling themselves from fragmented histories (Everything Everywhere, Aftersun, Past Lives). The formal ambition of the previous decade continued: directors willing to work at unusual lengths (Oppenheimer, Killers of the Flower Moon), in unusual structures (Tár), with unusual restraint (Past Lives, Aftersun). The best films of the decade so far trust their audiences completely.
