# The Best Films of the 1990s

## Why the 90s Mattered
The 1990s was the decade that gave birth to the modern independent film movement, produced the definitive action blockbusters, saw the full maturity of Hong Kong cinema before the handover, launched the Coen Brothers and Tarantino into the mainstream, and closed with a run of films — Fight Club, The Matrix, American Beauty, Eyes Wide Shut, Magnolia — that still define what "serious popular cinema" means.

## The Indie Revolution
Sundance and Miramax turned independent film into a commercial and cultural force:
- **sex, lies, and videotape (1989/1990)** — Rating: 7.2. Steven Soderbergh's debut. Won the Palme d'Or. Made Sundance matter.
- **Reservoir Dogs (1992)** — Rating: 8.3. Tarantino's debut. Changed what a movie could sound like.
- **Pulp Fiction (1994)** — Rating: 8.9. Changed what a movie could be structured like.
- **Clerks (1994)** — Rating: 7.7. Kevin Smith. Shot in a convenience store for $27,000. Launched a career.
- **Fargo (1996)** — Rating: 8.1. The Coens at their best.
- **Good Will Hunting (1997)** — Rating: 8.3. Gus Van Sant. Matt Damon and Ben Affleck's script. Robin Williams's defining dramatic performance.

## The Blockbuster Peak
- **Terminator 2 (1991)** — Rating: 8.6. The greatest action sequel ever made.
- **Jurassic Park (1993)** — Rating: 8.2. Spielberg. The dinosaurs still hold up. Changed CGI permanently.
- **The Lion King (1994)** — Rating: 8.5. Disney's peak. Hamlet for children.
- **Speed (1994)** — Rating: 7.3. A bomb on a bus. Pure invention.
- **Die Hard with a Vengeance (1995)** — Rating: 7.6. The best of the Die Hard sequels.
- **Mission: Impossible (1996)** — Rating: 7.1. De Palma. The fish tank sequence.
- **The Matrix (1999)** — Rating: 8.7. Changed cinema permanently.

## Crime and Drama
- **The Silence of the Lambs (1991)** — Rating: 8.6. Jodie Foster. Anthony Hopkins. The only horror film to win Best Picture.
- **Unforgiven (1992)** — Rating: 8.2. Clint Eastwood. The best western since Leone. Won Best Picture.
- **The Fugitive (1993)** — Rating: 7.8. Harrison Ford wrongly convicted of murder. Perfect studio thriller.
- **Schindler's List (1993)** — Rating: 9.0. The most important film of the decade.
- **The Shawshank Redemption (1994)** — Rating: 9.3. Frank Darabont. Stephen King adaptation. Consistently ranked the greatest film on IMDb.
- **Heat (1995)** — Rating: 8.3. Michael Mann. De Niro and Pacino across a table.
- **Se7en (1995)** — Rating: 8.6. The box.
- **L.A. Confidential (1997)** — Rating: 8.2. The best screenplay of the decade.
- **American History X (1998)** — Rating: 8.5. Edward Norton. A reformed neo-Nazi tries to save his brother.
- **Fight Club (1999)** — Rating: 8.8. David Fincher. Tyler Durden. The rules of Fight Club.

## Science Fiction
- **Terminator 2 (1991)** — Rating: 8.6.
- **Jurassic Park (1993)** — Rating: 8.2.
- **12 Monkeys (1995)** — Rating: 8.0. Terry Gilliam. Time travel and plague. Brad Pitt in a career-making supporting turn.
- **Contact (1997)** — Rating: 7.5. Jodie Foster. Carl Sagan adaptation. First contact as a story about faith.
- **Dark City (1998)** — Rating: 7.7. Underrated neo-noir science fiction. Pre-Matrix in many of its ideas.
- **The Matrix (1999)** — Rating: 8.7. Changes everything.
- **eXistenZ (1999)** — Rating: 6.8. Cronenberg's underrated VR thriller.
- **The Sixth Sense (1999)** — Rating: 8.2. M. Night Shyamalan. "I see dead people."

## International Cinema
- **Cinema Paradiso (1988/1990)** — Rating: 8.5. Tornatore. Nostalgia for cinema itself.
- **Raise the Red Lantern (1991)** — Rating: 8.1. Zhang Yimou. A woman becomes a rich man's concubine. Devastating.
- **Three Colors: Blue (1993)** — Rating: 8.1. Krzysztof Kieślowski. A woman rebuilds her life after her family is killed.
- **Chungking Express (1994)** — Rating: 8.1. Wong Kar-wai. Two love stories in Hong Kong. Beautiful.
- **Hana-bi (1997)** — Rating: 7.8. Takeshi Kitano. A retiring detective, violence, and painting.
- **Run Lola Run (1998)** — Rating: 7.7. Tom Tykwer. Twenty minutes, three times.

## The Year 1999: Cinema's Greatest Year?
1999 is often cited as the greatest single year in modern cinema:
- The Matrix (8.7), Fight Club (8.8), American Beauty (8.4), The Sixth Sense (8.2), Magnolia (8.0), Being John Malkovich (7.8), Eyes Wide Shut (7.4), Boys Don't Cry (7.6), All About My Mother (8.1), The Talented Mr. Ripley (7.4)

That is an extraordinary concentration of quality, ambition, and variety in a single twelve-month period.
