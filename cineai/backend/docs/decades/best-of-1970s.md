# The 1970s — The Golden Age of American Cinema

## Why the 70s Matter

The 1970s is the decade most film historians point to as the peak of American filmmaking. The collapse of the old studio system in the late 1960s created a power vacuum that a generation of film-school-educated directors rushed to fill. For roughly a decade, studios gave creative control to directors rather than producers, and the result was an explosion of formally ambitious, thematically dark, morally complex films that would have been impossible under the old system. Francis Ford Coppola, Martin Scorsese, Robert Altman, William Friedkin, Hal Ashby, Sidney Lumet, Brian De Palma, and others made their defining works in this decade.

## The Defining Films

### The Godfather (1972) — dir. Francis Ford Coppola ★★★★★
The greatest American film of the decade by most measures. Coppola's adaptation of Mario Puzo's novel transformed the gangster movie into a Shakespearean family tragedy. Marlon Brando as Vito Corleone, Al Pacino as Michael — the arc from reluctant outsider to ruthless patriarch is the decade's defining character journey. Gordon Willis's low-key lighting (the "Prince of Darkness") created images of extraordinary power. The film grossed more than any film in history to that point and won the Academy Award for Best Picture. Its sequel is one of the rare cases of a sequel surpassing the original.

### The Godfather Part II (1974) — dir. Francis Ford Coppola ★★★★★
Simultaneously a prequel and sequel, interweaving the rise of young Vito Corleone (Robert De Niro) in 1910s New York with Michael Corleone's (Al Pacino) consolidation of power in the 1950s. The two timelines comment on each other: Vito builds through loyalty and community; Michael destroys both. De Niro's performance — largely in Italian and Sicilian — is the finest of his career. The film won six Academy Awards including Best Picture, making Coppola the only director to win back-to-back for sequels.

### Chinatown (1974) — dir. Roman Polanski ★★★★★
The definitive neo-noir. Jack Nicholson as J.J. Gittes, a Los Angeles private detective, is hired for what seems a routine infidelity case and uncovers a conspiracy involving water rights, land fraud, and incest. Robert Towne's screenplay is one of the finest ever written — every scene contains information that will matter later. Polanski's ending is one of cinema's great refusals of hope: corruption wins, always has, always will. Faye Dunaway's performance as Evelyn Mulwray is heartbreaking. The film is a document of post-Watergate American despair.

### Apocalypse Now (1979) — dir. Francis Ford Coppola ★★★★★
The Vietnam War as Conrad's Heart of Darkness. Captain Willard (Martin Sheen) travels upriver to find and kill Colonel Kurtz (Marlon Brando), a decorated Special Forces officer who has gone rogue and built his own kingdom in Cambodia. Coppola filmed in the Philippines in conditions that became legendary for their difficulty — a near-fatal heart attack (Sheen), a typhoon destroying sets, Brando arriving overweight and unprepared, Dennis Hopper on drugs throughout. The making of the film is documented in Eleanor Coppola's Hearts of Darkness: A Filmmaker's Apocalypse (1991). The Ride of the Valkyries helicopter attack remains the most operatic sequence in American cinema.

### Taxi Driver (1976) — dir. Martin Scorsese ★★★★★
Travis Bickle (Robert De Niro), insomniac Vietnam veteran, drives a cab through New York's night streets. His alienation curdles into a messianic fantasy of violent purification. Bernard Herrmann's final score. Jodie Foster as Iris, the 12-year-old prostitute. Harvey Keitel. The climactic bloodbath was color-desaturated by Scorsese to soften the MPAA's rating objections, accidentally creating one of the most expressionistic sequences in American film. "You talkin' to me?" was improvised.

### Annie Hall (1977) — dir. Woody Allen ★★★★★
The film that established what the American romantic comedy could be. Alvy Singer (Allen) dissects the arc of his relationship with Annie Hall (Diane Keaton) in a non-linear, formally playful narrative that breaks the fourth wall, uses split screen, animated sequences, and subtitles to show inner thought. Won four Academy Awards including Best Picture and Best Director, defeating Star Wars — the last time a small, personal film would beat a blockbuster for the top prize until the 1990s.

### Star Wars (1977) — dir. George Lucas ★★★★
The film that ended the 70s as a director's decade and began the era of the blockbuster. Lucas drew on Joseph Campbell's Hero's Journey, Flash Gordon serials, Kurosawa's The Hidden Fortress, and WWII dogfight footage to create a myth for the modern era. John Williams' score. The practical effects. Alec Guinness's Obi-Wan Kenobi. The film's commercial success — the most profitable film of all time at its release — convinced studios that the money was in spectacle and franchise, not auteur cinema.

### Network (1976) — dir. Sidney Lumet ★★★★★
Paddy Chayefsky's screenplay predicted 24-hour news, reality television, the celebrity of outrage, and the commodification of rage with extraordinary precision. Peter Finch as Howard Beale, the "mad prophet of the airwaves." Faye Dunaway as Diana Christensen, television executive who programs Beale's breakdowns as entertainment. "I'm as mad as hell, and I'm not going to take this anymore!" Finch won a posthumous Academy Award.

### One Flew Over the Cuckoo's Nest (1975) — dir. Miloš Forman ★★★★★
Jack Nicholson as Randle McMurphy, who fakes insanity to serve his prison sentence in a mental institution and clashes with Nurse Ratched (Louise Fletcher). One of only three films to win all five major Academy Awards (Picture, Director, Actor, Actress, Screenplay). A film about institutional control, rebellion, and the cost of nonconformity.

### Jaws (1975) — dir. Steven Spielberg ★★★★
The first summer blockbuster. A great white shark terrorizes a beach resort. Spielberg's mechanical shark malfunctioned repeatedly, forcing him to suggest the shark rather than show it — one of the great accidental artistic decisions in cinema. Roy Scheider, Richard Dreyfuss, Robert Shaw. The Indianapolis speech. The film's commercial success alongside Star Wars established the summer as the primary season for film releases.

## The Directors of the Decade

**Robert Altman** made M*A*S*H (1970), McCabe & Mrs. Miller (1971), The Long Goodbye (1973), Nashville (1975) — his overlapping dialogue technique and ensemble casts created a cinema of democratic observation.

**Hal Ashby** made Harold and Maude (1971), The Last Detail (1973), Shampoo (1975), Coming Home (1978), Being There (1979) — humanist, anti-authoritarian, criminally underappreciated.

**William Friedkin** made The French Connection (1971) and The Exorcist (1973) back to back — one of the great two-year runs in cinema history.

---

## Legacy

The 70s established the template for what serious American cinema could be: director-driven, thematically ambitious, willing to end in darkness or ambiguity, interested in moral complexity rather than reassurance. Every subsequent generation of American filmmakers has defined themselves in relation to this decade.
