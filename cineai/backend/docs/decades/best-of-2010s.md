# The 2010s — Streaming, Prestige, and Global Cinema

## The Decade Defined

The 2010s saw the fragmentation of cinema's audience. Netflix's shift to original content (beginning with House of Cards in 2013) accelerated a migration of prestige storytelling from cinema to television. The Marvel Cinematic Universe consolidated the blockbuster into a franchise ecosystem that dominated box office. And simultaneously, some of the finest films ever made were produced — by auteurs who fought to preserve cinema as a space for adult, ambitious storytelling.

## The Essential Films

### The Tree of Life (2011) — dir. Terrence Malick ★★★★★
A family in 1950s Waco, Texas. A child's perspective on existence, grace, nature, and the cosmos. Malick intercutting domestic drama with the origins of the universe — the Big Bang, dinosaurs, the formation of Earth — is either the most audacious sequence in American cinema or the most pretentious, depending on your patience. Emmanuel Lubezki's cinematography. Brad Pitt and Jessica Chastain. Won the Palme d'Or.

### Moonlight (2016) — dir. Barry Jenkins ★★★★★
Three chapters in the life of Chiron, a Black boy growing up in Miami: childhood, adolescence, adulthood. Each chapter filmed differently, cast differently, paced differently. Jenkins' film is about identity, sexuality, tenderness, and the armor Black men are required to wear. Mahershala Ali's brief appearance as Juan won the Oscar. The beach scene is the most delicate moment in American cinema of the decade. Won Best Picture — memorably announced after La La Land was incorrectly named.

### Mad Max: Fury Road (2015) — dir. George Miller ★★★★★
A 70-year-old director returned to a franchise he created in 1979 and made the decade's most purely cinematic film. Two hours of chase across a post-apocalyptic desert. Charlize Theron as Furiosa — the film's actual protagonist, despite the title. Tom Hardy as Max. The practical effects, the in-camera stunts, the War Boys, the Doof Warrior playing flamethrower guitar on a moving vehicle. The film won six Academy Awards including editing and production design. A master class in action filmmaking.

### Her (2013) — dir. Spike Jonze ★★★★★
Theodore Twombly (Joaquin Phoenix) falls in love with an operating system (voiced by Scarlett Johansson). Jonze's film — set in a slightly-future Los Angeles of soft pastels and elevated walkways — is the decade's most prescient examination of loneliness, intimacy, and technology. The romance is entirely convincing. The ending, about the nature of consciousness expanding beyond human capacity for relationship, is quietly devastating.

### Parasite (2019) — dir. Bong Joon-ho ★★★★★
A poor Korean family infiltrates a wealthy family's household through a series of elaborate deceptions. Bong's film functions simultaneously as black comedy, thriller, and horror — its genre shifts are radical and perfectly executed. The revelation of the basement changes everything the audience thought they understood. Won four Academy Awards including Best Picture — the first non-English-language film to do so. One of the most precisely crafted screenplays ever written.

### Roma (2018) — dir. Alfonso Cuarón ★★★★★
A year in the life of a domestic worker (Yalitza Aparicio) in 1970s Mexico City, based on Cuarón's memories of his family's maid. Shot in black and white on digital by Cuarón himself. The film is formally extraordinary — long takes, precise blocking, a camera that pans back and forth observationally. The beach scene is one of the decade's defining moments. Won three Academy Awards. Released on Netflix, provoking debate about whether streaming films qualify as cinema.

### Get Out (2017) — dir. Jordan Peele ★★★★★
A Black man (Daniel Kaluuya) visits his white girlfriend's family. Jordan Peele's debut film uses horror genre conventions to examine racism with a specificity and wit that no previous film achieved. The hypnosis sequences, the Sunken Place, the auction scene. The film made $255 million on a $4.5 million budget. Peele became only the fifth person to receive Oscar nominations for Best Picture, Director, and Original Screenplay simultaneously.

### The Social Network (2010) — dir. David Fincher ★★★★★
Aaron Sorkin's screenplay about the founding of Facebook — structured as depositions from two simultaneous lawsuits — is one of the finest of the decade. Fincher's direction transforms corporate disputes into tragedy. Jesse Eisenberg's Mark Zuckerberg: the loneliest man in any room. Andrew Garfield as Eduardo Saverin. Trent Reznor and Atticus Ross's score. The rowing sequence. Won three Academy Awards.

### Arrival (2016) — dir. Denis Villeneuve ★★★★★
Twelve alien ships appear at various points on Earth. Amy Adams as the linguist attempting to communicate with them. Villeneuve's film — based on Ted Chiang's Story of Your Life — uses first contact science fiction to explore grief, non-linear time, and the nature of language. The structural reveal recontextualizes everything the audience believed about what they were watching.

## The Marvel Question

The MCU produced 23 films in the decade, grossing over $22 billion. Avengers: Endgame (2019) became the highest-grossing film of all time. Martin Scorsese described Marvel films as "not cinema" — as theme park rides rather than emotional experiences. The debate they provoked about what cinema is for, and who it is for, was the decade's defining film critical controversy.

---

## Television's Golden Age

The decade's most ambitious storytelling was increasingly on television: Breaking Bad (2008-2013), Game of Thrones (2011-2019), True Detective Season 1 (2014), The Americans (2013-2018), Atlanta (2016-2022), Fleabag (2016-2019), Succession (2018-2023). The line between cinema and television became the decade's most contested boundary.
