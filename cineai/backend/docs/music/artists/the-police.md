# The Police — Punk, Reggae, and Rock's Most Unlikely Superstar Band

## Formation and Members

The Police formed in London in 1977. Sting (Gordon Sumner, born 1951, Wallsend, Tyne and Wear) — vocals, bass. Andy Summers (born 1942, Poulton-le-Fylde, Lancashire) — guitar. Stewart Copeland (born 1952, Alexandria, Virginia) — drums. Copeland and Sting formed the initial lineup; Summers replaced original guitarist Henri Padovani in 1977.

The Police were a deliberate commercial creation at their origin — they bleached their hair blond for a Wrigley's chewing gum commercial. But the music they made was genuinely original: a synthesis of punk energy, reggae rhythm (Copeland's drumming was reggae-influenced, creating an unusual polyrhythmic feel in a rock context), jazz sophistication (Summers was a conservatory-trained guitarist), and Sting's literary lyrics.

## Discography

### Outlandos d'Amour (1978)
Recorded cheaply and quickly. Roxanne — about a prostitute, refused by BBC Radio on initial release, later a worldwide hit. Can't Stand Losing You. So Lonely. The album established their sound: reggae-inflected rock with distinctive three-way interplay.

### Reggatta de Blanc (1979)
Message in a Bottle — their first UK number one single, a song about isolation that became an ironic communal anthem. Reggatta de Blanc (White Reggae) won the Grammy for Best Rock Instrumental Performance. Walking on the Moon, The Bed's Too Big Without You. Won two Grammy Awards.

### Zenyatta Mondatta (1980)
Don't Stand So Close to Me (about a teacher's attraction to a student, UK number one), De Do Do Do, De Da Da Da (deceptively simple, about the power of nonsense over reason), Driven to Tears (about media indifference to global poverty). Won two Grammy Awards including Record of the Year for Don't Stand So Close to Me.

### Ghost in the Machine (1981)
Their most politically engaged album. Every Little Thing She Does Is Magic (UK number one), Invisible Sun (about the Troubles in Northern Ireland, the video was banned by the BBC), Spirits in the Material World. The album used saxophones and synthesizers more extensively than previous work.

### Synchronicity (1983)
Their masterpiece and final studio album. Every Breath You Take (one of the most commercially successful singles in rock history, often misread as romantic when it is actually about surveillance and possession), Wrapped Around Your Finger, King of Pain, Synchronicity I and II, Tea in the Sahara, Murder by Numbers. Won four Grammy Awards including Album of the Year and Record of the Year. The album spent 17 weeks at number one in the US.

## Breakup and Solo Careers

The Police broke up after the Synchronicity tour in 1984, primarily due to creative tensions between Sting and Copeland. Sting's solo career is one of the most commercially successful in rock history: The Dream of the Blue Turtles (1985, jazz-influenced, featuring Branford Marsalis), Nothing Like the Sun (1987), The Soul Cages (1991), Ten Summoner's Tales (1993), Brand New Day (1999).

Sting's music has appeared extensively in film and television. His acting career includes Quadrophenia (1979), Dune (1984), Brimstone & Treacle (1982), and Lock, Stock and Two Smoking Barrels (adjacent works).

Andy Summers pursued photography and occasional music projects. Stewart Copeland composed extensively for film and television, including the score for Rumble Fish (1983, dir. Francis Ford Coppola) and various other projects.

The Police reunited for a world tour in 2007-2008, grossing $358 million — one of the highest-grossing concert tours in history.

---

## Musical Influence

The Police's synthesis of reggae and rock was hugely influential on the ska revival of the late 1970s (Madness, The Specials) and on subsequent artists who blended reggae rhythm with rock structure. Sting's jazz-influenced bass playing and Copeland's polyrhythmic drumming created a template for sophisticated pop-rock that influenced artists from No Doubt to Maroon 5.

## Songs in Film and Television

- **Message in a Bottle** — referenced and used in numerous films and TV shows
- **Every Breath You Take** — used extensively in film and television, most notably Puff Daddy's sampling in "I'll Be Missing You" (1997 tribute to Notorious B.I.G.)
- **Don't Stand So Close to Me** — used in scenes about inappropriate relationships
- **Roxanne** — Moulin Rouge! (2001, cover version), numerous other uses
- **Driven to Tears** — political documentary contexts
