# Classic Rock — The Guitar Era

## What Classic Rock Is

Classic rock is broadly understood as rock music from the late 1960s through the late 1980s — the era of the guitar as the defining instrument of popular music, of the album as the primary artistic format, and of the rock concert as mass ritual. Its sources are American blues and British Invasion rock; its legacy is virtually every form of guitar-based popular music that followed.

## The Foundational Artists

### The Beatles (1960–1970)
The most important band in the history of popular music. John Lennon, Paul McCartney, George Harrison, and Ringo Starr transformed rock and roll from teen entertainment into art in a career spanning a decade and thirteen studio albums. Their progression — from Please Please Me (1963) to Abbey Road (1969) — compressed the equivalent of a century of musical development into seven years. Sgt. Pepper's Lonely Hearts Club Band (1967) established the concept album as a form. Their melodic invention, harmonic sophistication, and lyrical range remain unmatched.

### The Rolling Stones (1962–present)
The greatest rock and roll band, as distinct from the Beatles' pop/art achievement. Mick Jagger, Keith Richards, Charlie Watts, Brian Jones (until his death in 1969), Mick Taylor, Bill Wyman. The Stones' genius was sustained simplicity: Richards' guitar riffs (Satisfaction, Jumpin' Jack Flash, Brown Sugar, Start Me Up) are the most recognizable in rock. Their peak runs from Beggar's Banquet (1968) through Exile on Main St. (1972) — a four-year period of unmatched consistency in rock history.

### Led Zeppelin (1968–1980)
Jimmy Page, Robert Plant, John Paul Jones, John Bonham. The heaviest and most technically accomplished of the classic rock bands. Their synthesis of British folk, American blues, and hard rock created heavy metal as a form. Physical Graffiti (1975) is their masterpiece — a double album that encompasses everything the band could do. John Bonham's drumming on "When the Levee Breaks" (recorded in a stairwell for maximum reverb) is the most sampled drum track in hip-hop history. The band dissolved after Bonham's death in 1980.

### Bob Dylan (1961–present)
More properly a folk and rock artist than a classic rocker, but impossible to exclude. Dylan's electric conversion at Newport in 1965 — booed by folk purists — changed what rock could say. Highway 61 Revisited (1965) and Blonde on Blonde (1966) brought literary ambition to rock lyrics. "Like a Rolling Stone" — six minutes of accusation — is the most significant single in the form's history. His shadow falls over virtually every singer-songwriter who followed.

### The Who (1964–1982, intermittently after)
Roger Daltrey, Pete Townshend, John Entwistle, Keith Moon. Townshend's guitar technique (windmill strumming, power chords) and Moon's explosive drumming defined hard rock's physical energy. Tommy (1969) established the rock opera as a form. "Won't Get Fooled Again" (1971) is rock music's most powerful political statement. Moon's death in 1978 effectively ended the classic lineup.

### Jimi Hendrix (1966–1970)
The greatest electric guitarist in the history of rock. Hendrix's command of feedback, his improvisational ability, his integration of blues, psychedelia, and rhythm and blues created an approach to the electric guitar that has never been equaled. Are You Experienced (1967), Axis: Bold as Love (1967), Electric Ladyland (1968). His performance of "The Star-Spangled Banner" at Woodstock (1969) — using feedback to evoke air raids and napalm — is the most politically charged piece of rock performance. He died at 27.

### Pink Floyd (1965–1994)
Syd Barrett (until 1968), Roger Waters, David Gilmour, Richard Wright, Nick Mason. Their progression from psychedelic singles (See Emily Play) to extended suites (Echoes, Atom Heart Mother) to concept albums (The Dark Side of the Moon, Wish You Were Here, The Wall) defined the progressive rock tradition. The Dark Side of the Moon (1973) spent 741 weeks on the Billboard charts — 14 years. Waters' conceptual ambition and Gilmour's guitar playing (one of rock's most lyrical soloists) created a tension that produced their finest work.

### David Bowie (1969–2016)
The most theatrically inventive figure in rock history. Bowie's career is a series of complete reinventions: the folk singer of Space Oddity (1969), Ziggy Stardust (1972), Aladdin Sane (1973), the plastic soul of Young Americans (1975), the Berlin Trilogy (Low, Heroes, Lodger, 1977–1979 with Brian Eno), the pop of Let's Dance (1983). Heroes (1977) may be the greatest single song of the rock era. His final album Blackstar (2016), released two days before his death from cancer, addressed mortality directly.

### Bruce Springsteen (1973–present)
The definitive American rock artist. Born to Run (1975) established his myth: the working-class kid escaping the hometown, the open road, the girl, the car, the dream. Nebraska (1982) — stark acoustic demos recorded alone — stripped the myth back to its bones: murder, poverty, dead ends. Born in the USA (1984) was misread as patriotic anthem; it is a portrait of Vietnam veterans abandoned by the country they served.
