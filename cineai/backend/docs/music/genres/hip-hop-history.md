# Hip-Hop — The Most Important Music of the Last 50 Years

## Origins

Hip-hop emerged in the South Bronx in New York City in the early 1970s, created by Black and Latino youth in a borough that had been devastated by urban renewal, the withdrawal of city services, and arson (landlords burning buildings for insurance money). In this environment of economic abandonment, young people created a culture with four elements: DJing (manipulating records to create new music), MCing (rapping over the music), breakdancing, and graffiti.

DJ Kool Herc — Clive Campbell, a Jamaican immigrant — is credited with the founding act: isolating the percussion break in funk and soul records and extending it by switching between two copies of the same record. This "breakbeat" technique, developed in the South Bronx in 1973, is the technical origin of hip-hop. Afrika Bambaataa and Grandmaster Flash developed the DJ art further; the MCs who rhymed over the breaks became increasingly central.

## The Essential Eras

### Old School (1979–1986)
"Rapper's Delight" by the Sugarhill Gang (1979) was the first rap single to reach the mainstream. Grandmaster Flash and the Furious Five's "The Message" (1982) — "broken glass everywhere / people pissing on the stairs" — introduced social commentary to hip-hop. Run-DMC, with their rock/rap fusion and Adidas product placement, brought hip-hop to suburban white audiences.

### Golden Age (1986–1997)
The period of hip-hop's greatest artistic flowering. Public Enemy (Chuck D, Flavor Flav, Terminator X) made It Takes a Nation of Millions to Hold Us Back (1988) — the most politically radical popular music record of the decade, dense with sonic collage and Black nationalist rhetoric. N.W.A's Straight Outta Compton (1988) introduced gangsta rap — first-person narratives of street violence told with documentary specificity — and terrified mainstream America. Ice Cube, Dr. Dre, Eazy-E.

De La Soul, A Tribe Called Quest, and the Native Tongues collective offered an alternative — eclectic sampling, bohemian aesthetics, Afrocentric positivity. The Notorious B.I.G. (Biggie Smalls) and Jay-Z perfected the art of the New York street narrative. Nas's Illmatic (1994) — 39 minutes, 10 tracks, every bar essential — is the most concentrated argument for hip-hop as literature.

The East Coast/West Coast rivalry — centered on Biggie (Bad Boy Records, New York) and Tupac Shakur (Death Row Records, Los Angeles) — ended with both men murdered in 1996 and 1997. The violence their deaths represented, and the sorrow they occasioned, is one of American music's great tragedies.

### The Late 90s and 2000s
Jay-Z's Reasonable Doubt (1996) and The Blueprint (2001) established him as the dominant commercial and artistic figure of the era. Eminem — white rapper from Detroit — became the best-selling rap artist of the 2000s. Kanye West's College Dropout (2004) introduced soul-sample production and a different mode of rap vulnerability. Lil Wayne's mixtape dominance (The Carter III, 2008) redefined the rapper's relationship to the market.

### The 2010s and Beyond
Kendrick Lamar's good kid, m.A.A.d city (2012) and To Pimp a Butterfly (2015) established him as the most critically significant rapper of his generation — the latter a jazz-and-funk-inflected meditation on Black identity in America that many consider the greatest rap album of the decade. Drake's integration of R&B and rap, his emotional directness, and his streaming-era dominance made him the decade's most commercially successful figure. Childish Gambino, J. Cole, Tyler the Creator, Frank Ocean (who defies easy genre categorization).
