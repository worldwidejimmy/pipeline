# Jazz — America's Greatest Art Form

## What Jazz Is

Jazz is an American musical tradition that emerged in New Orleans in the early 20th century from the intersection of African American musical traditions — blues, ragtime, gospel, work songs — with European harmonic structures. Its defining characteristics are improvisation (musicians composing in real time within a structure), syncopation (emphasis on offbeats), swing feel, and collective conversation between instruments. It is simultaneously a music of extraordinary intellectual complexity and of visceral physical immediacy.

Jazz is America's most significant contribution to world music and one of the most important art forms of the 20th century. Its development from 1920 to 1975 roughly parallels the development of American cinema, and many of its greatest figures — Louis Armstrong, Duke Ellington, Charlie Parker, Miles Davis, John Coltrane — are among the most original artists of any medium in the 20th century.

## The Essential Eras

### New Orleans Jazz (1900–1920s)
Jazz emerged in the brothels and dance halls of New Orleans' Storyville district. Louis Armstrong (1901–1971) — born in New Orleans, raised in poverty, sent to reform school at 12 — became the music's first undisputed genius. His Hot Five and Hot Seven recordings (1925–1928) established improvised solo as the central act of jazz. Armstrong's trumpet tone, rhythmic invention, and melodic imagination transformed not just jazz but American popular music entirely. He moved to Chicago in 1922 and to New York in 1924, carrying the music with him.

### The Swing Era (1930s–1940s)
Big bands — 12 to 25 musicians — dominated American popular music in the 1930s and 40s. Duke Ellington (1899–1974), pianist and bandleader, built the finest orchestra in jazz history and composed for it for five decades. His Mood Indigo, Take the A Train, Sophisticated Lady, and hundreds of other compositions demonstrated that jazz could sustain large-scale compositional ambition without losing its improvisational soul. Benny Goodman, Count Basie, and Glenn Miller also led major orchestras. The swing era's dancers — the Lindy Hop at Harlem's Savoy Ballroom — created one of America's great vernacular art forms.

### Bebop (1940s)
A deliberate rejection of swing's commercial accessibility. Charlie Parker (1920–1955, alto saxophone) and Dizzy Gillespie (1917–1993, trumpet) developed bebop — fast tempos, complex harmonies, irregular phrase lengths — as a music for listening rather than dancing, and as an assertion of Black artistic seriousness against a music industry that gave white bandleaders the commercial rewards. Parker's recordings (Ko-Ko, Ornithology, A Night in Tunisia) are among the most technically demanding and emotionally direct music of the 20th century. Parker died at 34; a doctor examining the body estimated his age at 55.

### Cool Jazz and Hard Bop (1950s)
Miles Davis (1926–1991) brought jazz to a new audience in 1949–1950 with the Birth of the Cool sessions — a nonet that used unusual instrumentation (French horn, tuba) and arranged music with a reflective, spacious quality. The recordings influenced the West Coast cool school (Dave Brubeck, Chet Baker, Paul Desmond). On the East Coast, Art Blakey's Jazz Messengers developed hard bop — a return to blues and gospel roots with bop's harmonic sophistication — that became the dominant jazz tradition of the decade.

### Modal Jazz and Post-Bop (late 1950s–1960s)
Miles Davis's Kind of Blue (1959) is the best-selling jazz album in history and the most influential. Rather than complex chord progressions, Davis based the improvisation on modes (scales) — giving the soloists more space, creating music of unusual openness and serenity. The album featured John Coltrane (tenor saxophone), Bill Evans (piano), Cannonball Adderley (alto saxophone), Paul Chambers (bass), and Jimmy Cobb (drums).

John Coltrane (1926–1967) subsequently developed one of the most intense individual paths in jazz history. His A Love Supreme (1964) — a four-part suite expressing religious devotion — is one of the transcendent recordings of the 20th century. His later work (Ascension, 1965; Interstellar Space, 1967) pushed into free jazz territory before his death from liver cancer at 40.

### Free Jazz (1960s)
Ornette Coleman's The Shape of Jazz to Come (1959) and Free Jazz (1960) abandoned tonal centers and fixed tempos entirely. Coleman's alto saxophone — played with a plastic instrument — produced sounds of extraordinary rawness. Free jazz was controversial, divisive, and extraordinarily influential.

### Fusion (1970s)
Miles Davis's Bitches Brew (1970) electrified jazz — literally and metaphorically. Using electric keyboards, electric bass, and rock rhythms, Davis created a music that absorbed rock's energy without abandoning jazz's improvisational complexity. Weather Report (Joe Zawinul, Wayne Shorter), Herbie Hancock, and others developed fusion through the decade.
