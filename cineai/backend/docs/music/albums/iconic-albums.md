# Iconic Albums — The Records That Changed Music

## The Dark Side of the Moon — Pink Floyd (1973)

Recorded at Abbey Road Studios and released on 1 March 1973. The album is a continuous 43-minute work divided into two sides, exploring themes of time, mental illness, greed, conflict, and death. Roger Waters wrote most of the material; David Gilmour's guitar solos on The Great Gig in the Sky and Money are among rock's finest. Clare Torry's wordless vocal on The Great Gig in the Sky was improvised in a single take. Alan Parsons engineered the record; his use of tape loops, multitrack recording, and sound collage was state of the art.

The Dark Side of the Moon spent 741 non-consecutive weeks on the Billboard charts — more than 14 years. It has sold over 50 million copies worldwide, making it one of the best-selling albums in history. Its cover — a prism refracting white light into a spectrum, designed by Hipgnosis — is the most recognizable album cover in rock history.

## Thriller — Michael Jackson (1982)

Produced by Quincy Jones. Recorded at Westlake Studios, Los Angeles. Released November 30, 1982. The best-selling album in history, with certified sales of over 70 million copies. Nine tracks, seven top-ten singles in the United States — a commercial achievement that has never been equaled.

Billie Jean, Beat It (Eddie Van Halen's guitar solo), Wanna Be Startin' Somethin', Human Nature, PYT (Pretty Young Thing), The Girl Is Mine (with Paul McCartney), Thriller (with Vincent Price's spoken word passage). John Landis directed the 14-minute Thriller video — the most expensive music video ever made at the time, the most influential music video in history.

Jackson's vocal performances — the hiccups, the falsetto transitions, the rhythmic precision — represent the apex of pop vocal performance. Quincy Jones's production combined funk, R&B, rock, and pop with a clarity and energy that remains unmatched.

## Nevermind — Nirvana (1991)

Recorded with producer Butch Vig at Sound City Studios, Van Nuys, California, and released September 24, 1991 on DGC Records. Nevermind replaced Michael Jackson's Dangerous at number one on the Billboard charts in January 1992 — a seismic event that announced grunge's commercial arrival and the end of hair metal's dominance.

Kurt Cobain (vocals, guitar), Krist Novoselic (bass), Dave Grohl (drums, joining before the album's recording). Smells Like Teen Spirit, Come as You Are, Lithium, Polly, Territorial Pissings, Drain You, Lounge Act, Stay Away, On a Plain, Something in the Way.

Cobain's genius was for melody within noise — each Nirvana song has a melodic core of extraordinary catchiness that the distorted guitars amplify rather than obscure. The album's sound — loud/quiet dynamics, double-tracked guitars, Grohl's powerful drumming — defined 1990s alternative rock. Cobain's death by suicide in April 1994 made the album both a classic and an elegy.

## Graceland — Paul Simon (1986)

Recorded in Johannesburg, South Africa with Black South African musicians (the Boyoyo Boys, Ladysmith Black Mambazo, Los Lobos) and in New York and London. Released August 25, 1986. Simon's use of mbaqanga — township jive, the popular music of Black South African townships — created an album that sounded unlike anything in American popular music.

Graceland (the title track, a pilgrimage to Elvis's home), You Can Call Me Al (featuring a bass riff by Bakithi Kumalo that became one of pop music's most recognizable), Diamonds on the Soles of Her Shoes (with Ladysmith Black Mambazo), The Boy in the Bubble (about the simultaneity of technological wonder and global violence), Under African Skies, Homeless.

The album was controversial — South Africa was under apartheid, and many argued Simon was violating the cultural boycott of the regime. Simon argued he was supporting Black South African musicians and their music. Won Album of the Year Grammy. One of the most commercially successful and critically significant albums of the decade.

## Kind of Blue — Miles Davis (1959)

Recorded at Columbia's 30th Street Studio, New York, in two sessions: March 2 and April 22, 1959. Released August 17, 1959. The best-selling jazz album in history with over five million copies sold in the US alone.

Miles Davis (trumpet), John Coltrane (tenor saxophone), Cannonball Adderley (alto saxophone), Bill Evans (piano, on most tracks), Wynton Kelly (piano, on Freddie Freeloader), Paul Chambers (bass), Jimmy Cobb (drums).

Davis's modal approach — improvisation based on scales rather than chord changes — gave the musicians more melodic freedom and created music of unusual spaciousness and serenity. So What, Freddie Freeloader, Blue in Green, All Blues, Flamenco Sketches. The album was recorded with minimal rehearsal; some takes were first takes. The musicians were given sketches of scales and told to improvise. The result is simultaneously structured and free, composed and spontaneous.

## Born to Run — Bruce Springsteen (1975)

Recorded at 914 Sound Studios and the Record Plant, New York, released August 25, 1975. Springsteen and producer Jon Landau spent fourteen months making the album, perfecting the Wall of Sound production (influenced by Phil Spector) that would define Springsteen's classic period.

Thunder Road, Tenth Avenue Freeze-Out, Night, Backstreets, Born to Run, She's the One, Meeting Across the River, Jungleland. The Roy Bittan piano intro of Thunder Road, the saxophone of Clarence Clemons on Jungleland — these are among rock's most indelible sounds. Springsteen appeared simultaneously on the covers of Time and Newsweek in October 1975 — the only rock musician to do so — and the hype almost broke him.

Born to Run defined the mythology of American rock: the highway, the escape, the dream, the girl, the night. Its eight songs contain more cinematic imagery than most films.

## What's Going On — Marvin Gaye (1971)

Recorded at Hitsville U.S.A. (Studio A), Detroit, and Golden World Studio, Detroit. Released May 21, 1971 on Tamla Records. Gaye fought Motown head Berry Gordy to release the album — Gordy thought it too political and uncommercial. He was wrong.

What's Going On, What's Happening Brother, Flyin' High (In the Friendly Sky), Save the Children, God Is Love, Mercy Mercy Me (The Ecology), Right On, Wholy Holy, Inner City Blues (Make Me Wanna Holler). Gaye produced the album himself, creating a continuous suite structure in which songs blend into each other. His use of multitracked vocals — harmonizing with himself — created a new sound in soul music.

The album addressed the Vietnam War, poverty, police brutality, and environmental destruction. It remains the finest political album in American soul music and one of the greatest records of any genre.
