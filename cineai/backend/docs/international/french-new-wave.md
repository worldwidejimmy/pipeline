# The French New Wave — Cinema Reinvented

## What It Was

The French New Wave (Nouvelle Vague) was a film movement that emerged in France in the late 1950s and early 1960s, centered on a group of young critics writing for the journal Cahiers du Cinéma who became filmmakers. Their key insight was the auteur theory — the idea that the director is the primary author of a film, as the novelist is of a novel — which gave them a critical framework for valuing directors over studio productions and a creative framework for their own films. They made movies quickly and cheaply, on location, with handheld cameras and natural light, often improvising dialogue and allowing the materiality of filmmaking to show through: jump cuts, direct address, visible acknowledgments of the camera.

The movement's primary figures were Jean-Luc Godard, François Truffaut, Claude Chabrol, Jacques Rivette, and Éric Rohmer. Their films emerged in reaction against what they called "the cinema of quality" — the polished, literary, studio-bound French films of the 1940s and 1950s.

## The Essential Films

### The 400 Blows (Les Quatre Cents Coups, 1959) — dir. François Truffaut ★★★★★
Antoine Doinel (Jean-Pierre Léaud), a 12-year-old Parisian boy, is misunderstood by his parents and teachers and eventually sent to a reform school. Truffaut's debut feature — largely autobiographical — is the purest expression of the New Wave's emotional priorities: the child's perspective, the injustice of adult authority, the city as a space of freedom and danger. The final shot — Antoine running toward the sea and turning to look directly into the camera, freezing — is one of cinema's great endings: pure arrest, pure openness.

### Breathless (À bout de souffle, 1960) — dir. Jean-Luc Godard ★★★★★
Michel Poiccard (Jean-Paul Belmondo), small-time criminal, shoots a policeman and hides in Paris with his American girlfriend Patricia (Jean Seberg). Godard's debut feature, shot guerrilla-style on the streets of Paris, invented the jump cut as a deliberate formal choice — elisions of time within a continuous scene that acknowledged the constructedness of film narrative. Godard's film is also about American culture (Jean Seberg, Humphrey Bogart posters, jazz), about performance and authenticity, about betrayal. It transformed cinema.

### Contempt (Le Mépris, 1963) — dir. Jean-Luc Godard ★★★★★
Paul Javal (Michel Piccoli), a screenwriter hired to work on a film adaptation of The Odyssey being produced by an American (Jack Palance) and directed by Fritz Lang (playing himself), watches his marriage to Camille (Brigitte Bardot) collapse. Godard's most accessible film and possibly his greatest — a meditation on cinema itself, using Fritz Lang's physical presence to ask what film has been and what it has become. Raoul Coutard's 'Scope cinematography. Georges Delerue's score. "The cinema is not a slice of life, but a piece of cake." — Godard/Hitchcock.

### Hiroshima Mon Amour (1959) — dir. Alain Resnais ★★★★★
A French actress (Emmanuelle Riva) and a Japanese architect (Eiji Okada) have an affair in Hiroshima. Their conversation — intercut with documentary footage of the atomic bomb's aftermath — is an exploration of memory, trauma, and the impossibility of fully possessing another person's experience. Marguerite Duras wrote the screenplay. The film's editing — associative, memory-based — influenced every subsequent filmmaker who has tried to represent trauma on screen.

### Vivre sa Vie (My Life to Live, 1962) — dir. Jean-Luc Godard ★★★★★
Twelve episodes in the life of Nana (Anna Karina), who becomes a prostitute. Godard's film treats its subject with both political distance (intertitles, economic data, a philosopher's discussion of language and freedom) and intense emotional proximity. Karina's performance — she and Godard were married at the time — is one of cinema's most affecting. The scene in which Nana weeps watching The Passion of Joan of Arc.

### Cléo from 5 to 7 (1962) — dir. Agnès Varda ★★★★★
A pop singer (Corinne Marchand) waits for the results of a cancer test, spending two hours wandering Paris in real time. Varda — the only woman of the New Wave's core — made a film about mortality, vanity, and the female experience of being looked at. The film's shift from subjective to objective framing (from Cléo being seen to Cléo seeing) is one of cinema's most precisely executed formal arguments.

### Jules and Jim (1962) — dir. François Truffaut ★★★★★
Two friends — Jules (Oskar Werner) and Jim (Henri Serre) — both fall in love with the same woman, Catherine (Jeanne Moreau), over several decades. Truffaut's film is about the impossibility of love as possession, about the forms of friendship that transcend romantic competition, and about the devastation of time. Georges Delerue's score. Jeanne Moreau's Catherine — mercurial, anarchic, entirely herself — is one of cinema's great female characters.

### Weekend (1967) — dir. Jean-Luc Godard ★★★★★
A bourgeois couple drive through the French countryside; the journey becomes an apocalyptic traffic jam of wrecked cars and corpses. Godard's angriest film, made just before May '68, is a comprehensive assault on capitalism, consumerism, and the violence at the heart of bourgeois civilization. The 10-minute lateral tracking shot past a traffic jam is one of cinema's most dazzling long takes.

## Legacy

The New Wave's influence on subsequent cinema is impossible to overstate. Martin Scorsese, Francis Ford Coppola, Brian De Palma, Steven Spielberg, and the entire New Hollywood generation learned from the New Wave that cinema could be personal, formally experimental, and emotionally direct simultaneously. Godard's formal experiments continue to influence filmmakers. Varda's humanist documentary approach shaped observational cinema. Truffaut's Antoine Doinel — appearing in five films across twenty years — established the model for the autobiographical film cycle.
