# Korean Cinema — The World's Most Exciting Film Industry

## The Context

Korean cinema emerged as a world force in the 2000s, driven by a generation of directors who trained in Korean film schools and combined Hollywood genre fluency with a distinctly Korean sensibility: acute class consciousness, formal audacity, willingness to mix tones (comedy and horror and tragedy can coexist in a single scene), and a political engagement rooted in Korea's traumatic 20th century (Japanese occupation, the Korean War, military dictatorship until 1987). The 2019 Oscar success of Parasite announced to international audiences what Korean cinema enthusiasts had known for two decades.

## The Essential Films

### Oldboy (2003) — dir. Park Chan-wook ★★★★★
Oh Dae-su is imprisoned for fifteen years without explanation, then released. His search for his captor leads to revelations that are among the most disturbing in cinema. Park's second entry in his Vengeance Trilogy (following Sympathy for Mr. Vengeance, preceding Lady Vengeance) is the definitive Korean genre film — a work of operatic violence, formal invention, and genuine tragedy. The corridor fight sequence — a long, exhausted, lateral battle — is one of the great action scenes in cinema: brutal because the hero can barely stand.

### Memories of Murder (2003) — dir. Bong Joon-ho ★★★★★
The investigation of South Korea's first documented serial murders, in rural Gyeonggi Province in the 1980s. Bong's film is simultaneously a police procedural, a dark comedy about provincial incompetence, and an elegy for the failures of institutions. The killer is never identified. Song Kang-ho as the lead detective — physically expressive, funny, heartbreaking — is one of cinema's great performances.

### A Tale of Two Sisters (2003) — dir. Kim Jee-woon ★★★★★
Two sisters return home after time in a psychiatric institution to find their stepmother increasingly threatening. Korean horror at its most formally precise — the film uses unreliable narration and spatial disorientation to create genuine dread. The reveal rewrites everything.

### The Host (2006) — dir. Bong Joon-ho ★★★★★
A creature emerges from the Han River and takes the daughter of a family of river snack-bar vendors. Bong's monster movie is also a class comedy, an anti-American political allegory, and a family drama. The sequence in which the family mourns the daughter's presumed death — laughing and wailing simultaneously, rolling on the floor — is one of the most precisely observed grief scenes in cinema.

### Mother (2009) — dir. Bong Joon-ho ★★★★★
A mother's intellectually disabled son is accused of murdering a local girl. She investigates to clear him. Bong's film — relentlessly moral, formally precise — is about the violence of maternal love and the moral catastrophes we commit in the name of protection. Kim Hye-ja's performance is extraordinary.

### I Saw the Devil (2010) — dir. Kim Jee-woon ★★★★
A special agent (Lee Byung-hun) pursues the serial killer (Choi Min-sik) who murdered his fiancée, choosing not to kill him but to repeatedly find, defeat, and release him — to make him suffer. The film is about the corrupting effect of revenge on the avenger. Deeply violent, morally serious.

### The Wailing (2016) — dir. Na Hong-jin ★★★★★
A mysterious illness spreads through a rural village after a Japanese stranger arrives. Na Hong-jin's film — two hours and thirty-six minutes, an accumulation of dread and bewilderment — withholds its explanations until an ending that refuses comfort. What is the stranger? What is the shaman? The film insists on uncertainty as its subject.

### Burning (2018) — dir. Lee Chang-dong ★★★★★
A young man (Yoo Ah-in) falls for a woman (Jeon Jong-seo) who returns from Africa with a mysterious new friend (Steven Yeun) who confesses a peculiar hobby. Based on Haruki Murakami's story Barn Burning. Lee's film is about class, desire, and masculine inadequacy — told with a patience and ambiguity that leaves the central event permanently unresolved. Steven Yeun's performance as the enigmatic Ben is one of the decade's finest.

### Parasite (2019) — dir. Bong Joon-ho ★★★★★
See: 2010s Guide. The Kim family's infiltration of the Park household. Class, architecture, the smell of money. Four Academy Awards including Best Picture. The film that brought Korean cinema to its widest international audience.

## The Directors

**Bong Joon-ho**: The most internationally successful Korean director. Memories of Murder, The Host, Mother, Snowpiercer (2013), Okja (2017), Parasite — a filmography of remarkable consistency and ambition.

**Park Chan-wook**: The Vengeance Trilogy, Sympathy for Lady Vengeance (2005), Thirst (2009), The Handmaiden (2016), Decision to Leave (2022) — a cinema of extreme aestheticism and moral complexity.

**Lee Chang-dong**: Peppermint Candy (1999), Oasis (2002), Secret Sunshine (2007), Poetry (2010), Burning (2018) — perhaps the most purely humanist of Korean directors, less genre-inflected, more concerned with individual suffering.

**Hong Sang-soo**: A prolific director of small, formally repetitive films about intellectuals, artists, and romantic failure. His films resist summary but reward sustained attention.
