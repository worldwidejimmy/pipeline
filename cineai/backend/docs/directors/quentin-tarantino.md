# Quentin Tarantino — Director Guide

## Overview
Quentin Tarantino (born 1963) is the most influential American filmmaker of the 1990s and one of the most distinctive voices in world cinema. A former video store clerk with no formal film school training, he rewrote the rules of indie cinema with Reservoir Dogs, then permanently changed popular filmmaking with Pulp Fiction. His work is defined by non-linear storytelling, explosive violence treated as black comedy, dialogue that turns characters' voices into music, and a deep encyclopedic love of cinema history that turns every film into a conversation with the movies he loves.

## Style and Technique
- **Non-linear narrative** — Pulp Fiction, Kill Bill, The Hateful Eight all scramble chronology to produce shock and irony
- **Dialogue as texture** — characters talk about pop culture, food, and trivialities before or instead of the plot; the talk is the point
- **Chapter titles and title cards** — structures films as novels
- **Long takes and slow-burn tension** — The Hateful Eight's opening 45 minutes; the Sicilian speech in True Romance
- **Foot fetishism** — a well-documented leitmotif across his filmography
- **Trunk shots** and low angles as signature framings
- **Needle drops over score** — he finds existing songs rather than commissioning music

## Themes
- Revenge as ritual and catharsis (Kill Bill, Django Unchained, Inglourious Basterds)
- Pop culture as the lens through which modern Americans understand everything
- The ethics of violence — his films luxuriate in it while making you uncomfortable about your enjoyment
- Loyalty, betrayal, and honour among criminals and outsiders
- Revisionist history as wish fulfillment (Inglourious Basterds, Once Upon a Time in Hollywood)

## Key Films

**Reservoir Dogs (1992)** — Rating: 8.3/10
Debut feature. A diamond heist told entirely in aftermath. Famous for the ear-cutting scene that you barely see. Introduced his ear for dialogue and his structural confidence.

**Pulp Fiction (1994)** — Rating: 8.9/10
Three interweaving stories about Los Angeles criminals. Won the Palme d'Or. Revived John Travolta's career. Changed independent cinema permanently. One of the most quoted films ever made. The gimp, the overdose, the Royale with Cheese — it's become part of the cultural air.

**Jackie Brown (1997)** — Rating: 7.5/10
His most underrated film. An adaptation of Elmore Leonard's Rum Punch. Pam Grier as a middle-aged flight attendant who outsmarts everyone. More melancholy and tender than his other work.

**Kill Bill: Volume 1 (2003)** — Rating: 8.2/10
Revenge fantasy structured as a samurai film, martial arts movie, anime, and spaghetti western. Uma Thurman's Bride on a vengeance mission. The Crazy 88 fight sequence is a pure action set-piece.

**Kill Bill: Volume 2 (2004)** — Rating: 8.0/10
Slower and more emotional companion to Volume 1. More dialogue, more character. The buried-alive sequence. David Carradine's monologue about Superman.

**Inglourious Basterds (2009)** — Rating: 8.4/10
WWII revisionist fantasy. Christoph Waltz's Hans Landa is one of cinema's great villains. The basement bar sequence is among the most suspenseful scenes ever made — entirely through conversation.

**Django Unchained (2012)** — Rating: 8.5/10
Southern revisionist western. A freed slave and a German bounty hunter team up. Jamie Foxx and Christoph Waltz. Controversial for its treatment of slavery but viscerally satisfying as genre entertainment.

**The Hateful Eight (2015)** — Rating: 7.8/10
Wintery chamber western. Eight strangers in a stagecoach stopover. Shot in Ultra Panavision 70mm. Slow and claustrophobic by design, then explosively violent.

**Once Upon a Time in Hollywood (2019)** — Rating: 7.6/10
His most personal film. 1969 Los Angeles. A fading TV actor and his stuntman navigate a changing industry. Nostalgic, melancholy, and ultimately a fantasy of how things could have gone differently.

## Legacy
Tarantino proved that a cinema obsessive with no industry background could make films that changed the industry. Every indie filmmaker since the mid-90s has had to reckon with his influence on dialogue, structure, and the ethics of genre violence.

- Park Chan-wook (revenge as formal art — Oldboy, The Handmaiden)
