# Steven Spielberg — Director Guide

## Overview
Steven Spielberg (born 1946) is the most commercially successful filmmaker in history and, alongside George Lucas, the inventor of the modern blockbuster. But the blockbuster reputation obscures a more complex figure: a director of tremendous emotional intelligence who has also made some of the most devastating serious films of the last fifty years (Schindler's List, Munich, Saving Private Ryan). He works in almost every genre and has never made an uninteresting film.

## Visual Style
- **The Spielberg face** — a close-up of a character's face reacting with wonder, fear, or awe, used as punctuation throughout his films
- **Backlighting** — figures silhouetted against light sources (E.T.'s bicycle, Close Encounters' ship)
- **The dolly zoom (Vertigo effect)** — famously used in Jaws, later adopted everywhere
- **Long lenses compressing space** — the beach landing in Saving Private Ryan puts you inside the chaos
- **Child's-eye view** — low angles, the world seen from below, in his family films

## Themes
- Fathers, absent fathers, and surrogate families (E.T., Indiana Jones, Hook, Catch Me If You Can)
- The wonder of the unknown and humanity's capacity for awe (Close Encounters, E.T., 1941)
- Evil as banal bureaucracy (Schindler's List, Bridge of Spies, Munich)
- The American experience — its mythology and its failures
- War as simultaneously heroic and horrifying (Saving Private Ryan, Dunkirk, Empire of the Sun)

## Key Films

**Jaws (1975)** — Rating: 8.0/10
The first modern blockbuster. A shark terrorises a beach town. Shot against enormous difficulties (the mechanical shark rarely worked, forcing Spielberg to suggest rather than show). Set the template for summer movies. Still terrifying.

**Close Encounters of the Third Kind (1977)** — Rating: 7.6/10
A power-line worker becomes obsessed with a vision of a mountain after a UFO encounter. Spielberg's most personal film of the 70s — about the terror and joy of contact with something beyond human understanding.

**Raiders of the Lost Ark (1981)** — Rating: 8.4/10
The most purely entertaining adventure film ever made. Harrison Ford as Indiana Jones. Designed as a tribute to the Saturday morning serials of Spielberg and Lucas's childhood. Every action setpiece is a masterclass.

**E.T. the Extra-Terrestrial (1982)** — Rating: 7.9/10
A lonely boy befriends a stranded alien. The most successful film of its era. The bicycle silhouette against the moon is cinema's most recognisable image. Genuinely affecting even now.

**The Color Purple (1985)** — Rating: 7.8/10
Adaptation of Alice Walker's novel. Spielberg's first serious dramatic film, about a Black woman's life in the American South across four decades. Underrated.

**Empire of the Sun (1987)** — Rating: 7.8/10
A British boy is separated from his parents in Shanghai during WWII. Christian Bale's debut. About the death of innocence and the strange, amoral beauty that children find in catastrophe.

**Schindler's List (1993)** — Rating: 9.0/10
The story of Oskar Schindler, a Nazi profiteer who saved over a thousand Jewish lives during the Holocaust. Shot in black and white. Three and a half hours. Probably the most important film about the Holocaust ever made. The girl in the red coat. The final list scene. Essential viewing.

**Saving Private Ryan (1998)** — Rating: 8.6/10
The D-Day landing sequence (the first 27 minutes) is the most viscerally terrifying war sequence ever committed to film. The rest is a moral fable about who is worth saving and why. Tom Hanks gives his best performance.

**A.I. Artificial Intelligence (2001)** — Rating: 7.2/10
A collaboration with Kubrick, who died before production. A robot boy programmed to love is abandoned by his human family. Misunderstood on release; increasingly seen as one of the great science fiction films.

**Minority Report (2002)** — Rating: 7.7/10
Pre-crime police thriller. Tom Cruise as a cop in a future where murders are prevented before they happen. Tense, clever, prescient about surveillance technology.

**Munich (2005)** — Rating: 7.6/10
The secret Israeli operation to hunt down the perpetrators of the 1972 Olympic massacre. Morally serious and genuinely disturbing about the cost of revenge.

**Lincoln (2012)** — Rating: 7.4/10
Daniel Day-Lewis as Abraham Lincoln in the final months of his presidency, pushing the 13th Amendment through Congress. About political compromise as a moral act.

## Legacy
Spielberg invented the grammar of popular cinema as we know it. Nearly every blockbuster made since 1975 owes a debt to Jaws, and nearly every emotional crowd-pleaser owes one to E.T. The more interesting question is whether Schindler's List, Saving Private Ryan, and Munich will end up as his most important films.

## For Fans Of Spielberg, Try
- Robert Zemeckis (Back to the Future — cut from the same cloth)
- J.J. Abrams (Super 8 is a direct tribute)
- John Ford (the American mythmaking Spielberg learned from)
- George Miller (Mad Max: Fury Road for pure kinetic blockbuster craft)
