# Paul Thomas Anderson — America's Greatest Living Director

## The Director

Paul Thomas Anderson (PTA) is widely considered the finest American director working today. Born in 1970 in Studio City, California — the son of a character actor — he absorbed cinema from childhood and made his first short films as a teenager. His features are long, formally ambitious, emotionally overwhelming, and rooted in American mythology: the San Fernando Valley, the California oil fields, the cults and confidence men of American spiritual entrepreneurship. He works with recurring collaborators — Philip Seymour Hoffman appeared in five of his first six films — and has developed one of the most consistent and recognizable bodies of work in contemporary cinema.

## The Films

### Hard Eight (1996) ★★★★
An older gambler (Philip Baker Hall) takes a young drifter (John C. Reilly) under his wing. PTA's debut feature, made for $3 million, establishes the themes he will return to throughout his career: father-son surrogates, loyalty, shame, the codes of male relationship.

### Boogie Nights (1997) ★★★★★
The San Fernando Valley pornography industry from 1977 to 1984. Dirk Diggler (Mark Wahlberg), a teenager with an extraordinary physical gift, is discovered by Jack Horner (Burt Reynolds), an ambitious director who believes pornography can be art. The film charts the rise and fall of a surrogate family — the ensemble includes Julianne Moore, Don Cheadle, John C. Reilly, Philip Seymour Hoffman, William H. Macy — and uses the transition from film to video as a metaphor for the collapse of 70s idealism. The opening Steadicam shot, moving from parking lot to nightclub interior, is one of the great opening shots in American cinema.

### Magnolia (1999) ★★★★★
Nine characters in the San Fernando Valley on one day, their stories converging toward a mysterious shared catastrophe. PTA's most formally ambitious early film — three hours and eight minutes, an ensemble of extraordinary density: Tom Cruise as a motivational guru for men (his finest performance), Philip Baker Hall as a dying game show host, Julianne Moore as his wife, Jason Robards as his cancer-ridden father, William H. Macy, John C. Reilly, Philip Seymour Hoffman. The sequence in which all characters simultaneously sing along to Aimee Mann's "Wise Up" is one of cinema's most audacious formal experiments. The frogs.

### Punch-Drunk Love (2002) ★★★★★
Barry Egan (Adam Sandler) — timid, overwhelmed, prone to violent outbursts — falls in love with Lena Leonard (Emily Watson) while being extorted by a phone sex mattress salesman (Philip Seymour Hoffman). PTA's smallest film and perhaps his most perfect. Sandler's performance — a complete departure from his comedic persona — is extraordinary. Jon Brion's score. The Hawaiian shirts. A film about how love can constitute survival.

### There Will Be Blood (2007) ★★★★★
Daniel Plainview (Daniel Day-Lewis), oil prospector, rises to become a ruthless oil baron in California at the turn of the 20th century, with Eli Sunday (Paul Dano), evangelical preacher, as his nemesis. Day-Lewis's performance — the finest of his career, arguably the finest film performance in American cinema of the 21st century — creates a character of Shakespearean magnitude. Jonny Greenwood's score (the first time a Radiohead member scored a Hollywood film) is dissonant, visceral, perfectly matched to the material. "I'm finished!" The milkshake speech. The film's opening 15 minutes contain no dialogue.

### The Master (2012) ★★★★★
Freddie Quell (Joaquin Phoenix), a damaged WWII veteran, falls under the influence of Lancaster Dodd (Philip Seymour Hoffman), the charismatic founder of a philosophical movement ("The Cause") that closely resembles Scientology. The film is less about the cult than about the relationship between Freddie and Lancaster — whether it is father-son, romantic, symbiotic, or all three simultaneously. Mihai Mălaimare Jr.'s 65mm cinematography is the most beautiful of the decade.

### Inherent Vice (2014) ★★★★
Doc Sportello (Joaquin Phoenix), hippie private eye, navigates a labyrinthine conspiracy in late-60s/early-70s Los Angeles. Adapted from Thomas Pynchon's novel. A stoned elegy for the counterculture — "the Psychedelic Finks," as Doc thinks of the forces of Nixon's America that have absorbed the Summer of Love and turned it into real estate. Josh Brolin. Benicio del Toro. Owen Wilson. Katherine Waterston.

### Phantom Thread (2017) ★★★★★
Reynolds Woodcock (Daniel Day-Lewis) — a fastidious, controlling dressmaker in 1950s London — meets Alma (Vicky Krieps), a waitress who becomes his muse and, in time, his match. Day-Lewis announced his retirement after this film. Jonny Greenwood's score. The film's final revelation — what Alma does to Reynolds to maintain equality in their relationship — reframes everything. A film about power, nourishment, and the terms on which difficult people can be loved.

### Licorice Pizza (2021) ★★★★★
Alana Kane (Alana Haim) and Gary Valentine (Cooper Hoffman, Philip Seymour Hoffman's son) — fifteen years apart in age — run through the San Fernando Valley in 1973. PTA's most joyful film. A love story that refuses to let its age-gap premise become either romantic or scandalous. The sequence with Sean Penn and Tom Waits. Bradley Cooper as Jon Peters. Running — characters are always running in this film, toward something that keeps moving.

## Collaborators

**Daniel Day-Lewis**: There Will Be Blood, The Master, Phantom Thread — the finest director-actor trilogy of the century.
**Jonny Greenwood**: Scored There Will Be Blood, The Master, Inherent Vice, Phantom Thread, Licorice Pizza — one of the great director-composer partnerships.
**Philip Seymour Hoffman**: Hard Eight, Boogie Nights, Magnolia, Punch-Drunk Love, The Master — a partnership that ended with Hoffman's death in 2014.
