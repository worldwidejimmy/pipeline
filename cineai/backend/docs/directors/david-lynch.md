# David Lynch — The American Unconscious

## The Director

David Lynch is the most genuinely surrealist filmmaker in the history of American cinema. Born in Missoula, Montana in 1946, trained as a painter at the Pennsylvania Academy of Fine Arts, he came to film as a visual artist and has never entirely abandoned that sensibility. His films work through image and sound rather than narrative logic — they produce states of feeling rather than stories. He has said he does not know what his films mean, and this is not false modesty: they arise from intuition, dream logic, and what he calls "ideas" that arrive fully formed during meditation (he is a longtime practitioner of Transcendental Meditation).

Lynch's consistent subject is the rupture of American normalcy — the white picket fence world of small towns and suburban comfort that conceals violence, sexual pathology, and terror. His films do not explain this rupture; they inhabit it.

## The Films

### Eraserhead (1977) ★★★★★
Henry Spencer (Jack Nance) lives in an industrial wasteland, has a mutant baby with his girlfriend, and experiences a series of nightmarish visions. Shot over five years on a budget assembled from small grants, Eraserhead is one of the most original debut films in cinema history. It has no precedent. The sound design — by Lynch, developed with Alan Splet — is its own character. Stanley Kubrick reportedly screened it for his cast before making The Shining. Its tonal combination of domestic anxiety and cosmic horror established the Lynch universe.

### The Elephant Man (1980) ★★★★★
John Merrick (John Hurt), severely deformed, is rescued from a Victorian freak show by surgeon Frederick Treves (Anthony Hopkins). Lynch's only conventionally narrative film, and a masterpiece within those conventions. Shot in black and white by Freddie Francis. The contrast between Merrick's physical appearance and his inner refinement is the film's engine. "I am not an animal! I am a human being! I... am... a man!"

### Blue Velvet (1986) ★★★★★
See: 1980s Guide. Jeffrey Beaumont finds a severed ear and descends into a criminal underworld. Dennis Hopper as Frank Booth. Isabella Rossellini. The film that established Lynch as a major filmmaker and introduced his central metaphor: the American lawn and what lives beneath it.

### Wild at Heart (1990) ★★★★
Sailor Ripley (Nicolas Cage) and Lula Fortune (Laura Dern) — deeply in love and deeply weird — drive across America pursued by Lula's mother and her associates. A road movie filtered through The Wizard of Oz. Won the Palme d'Or. The most overtly comic of Lynch's films.

### Twin Peaks: Fire Walk with Me (1992) ★★★★★
The final days of Laura Palmer before her murder — the event that the television series Twin Peaks investigates from the other direction. A difficult, harrowing, often unwatchable (in the best sense) film about sexual abuse, dissociation, and the failure of institutions to protect children. Booed at Cannes. Cheryl Lee's performance as Laura is one of cinema's most overlooked.

### Lost Highway (1997) ★★★★
A musician (Bill Pullman) receives mysterious videotapes, is convicted of his wife's murder, and somehow transforms into a different person (Balthazar Getty) in prison. The film does not resolve its central mystery — identity, guilt, the unreliable narrator of the self. Patricia Arquette in two roles. A film about the stories we tell ourselves to survive.

### The Straight Story (1999) ★★★★★
An elderly man (Richard Farnsworth) rides a lawnmower across Iowa to visit his ailing brother. Lynch's only G-rated film, and his most moving. Shot on location across the Midwest by Freddie Francis. The film strips away everything Lynch is known for — no violence, no surrealism, no mystery — and reveals the humanism beneath. Farnsworth's performance, made as he was dying of cancer (he took his own life shortly after the film's release), is unbearable in its honesty.

### Mulholland Drive (2001) ★★★★★
Betty Elms (Naomi Watts), aspiring actress, arrives in Hollywood and finds an amnesiac woman (Laura Harring) hiding in her apartment. The first half functions as a cheerful mystery; the second half — after the blue box — retroactively reveals the first half as fantasy. The film is about Hollywood itself: the dream it sells and the reality it conceals, the gap between the actress who arrives and the actress who survives. Widely regarded as Lynch's masterpiece. Roger Ebert's favorite film of the decade.

### Inland Empire (2006) ★★★★
An actress (Laura Dern) becomes increasingly confused between her role and her identity while filming a cursed movie. Shot on consumer-grade digital video, fragmentary and nearly three hours long. Lynch's most difficult film and also his most formally radical — a film about the nature of fiction, performance, and the self.

## Twin Peaks (Television)

Lynch's television work is as significant as his films. Twin Peaks (1990-1991) co-created with Mark Frost transformed American television — its mixture of soap opera, mystery, and surrealism anticipated prestige television by two decades. Twin Peaks: The Return (2018), 18 hours on Showtime, is arguably the greatest single thing Lynch has made: a meditation on time, America, the nature of evil, and the impossibility of going home.

## Sound

Lynch is as important a sound designer as he is a director. The industrial hums, distorted voices, and ambient drones that pervade his work are created by Lynch himself, and they are as responsible for the films' effects as the images. His collaboration with composer Angelo Badalamenti produced some of the most distinctive film music of the past 40 years.
