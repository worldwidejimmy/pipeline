# Martin Scorsese — Director Study

## Overview

Martin Scorsese (b. 1942, Queens, NY) is America's greatest living filmmaker. Over six decades he has defined the gangster film, revitalized classical Hollywood cinema, and created some of the most morally complex character studies in cinema history. His work is inseparable from the New Hollywood movement of the 1970s — a period when a generation of film-school graduates remade American cinema in the image of European art film.

**Key films:** Mean Streets (1973), Taxi Driver (1976), Raging Bull (1980), The King of Comedy (1982), GoodFellas (1990), Casino (1995), Gangs of New York (2002), The Aviator (2004), The Departed (2006), Shutter Island (2010), Hugo (2011), The Wolf of Wall Street (2013), Silence (2016), The Irishman (2019), Killers of the Flower Moon (2023)

## Signature Style

### Camera as Moral Agent
Scorsese's camera doesn't simply record — it participates. Slow motion in Raging Bull turns violence into ballet and horror simultaneously. The long Copacabana tracking shot in GoodFellas (Henry Hill and Karen entering through the kitchen) is conducted from Karen's point of view — we experience her seduction by this world exactly as she does, the camera gliding past deference and privilege, making the criminal life glamorous before the film tears it apart. Freeze frames punctuate GoodFellas like moral punctuation marks.

### Rock and Pop as Score
Scorsese pioneered the use of pre-existing rock and pop music as film score. "Gimme Shelter" appears in GoodFellas, Casino, and The Departed — each time it signals violence and social chaos. The opening of GoodFellas cuts to "Rags to Riches." "Layla" plays over the bodies. The music tells you what to feel before you've processed what you're seeing, then the image complicates the feeling.

### The Unreliable Protagonist
Scorsese's narrators lie to themselves. Henry Hill in GoodFellas is proud of the violence and complicit in it. Jordan Belfort in The Wolf of Wall Street is a monster who describes himself as a victim. Jake LaMotta in Raging Bull is destroying himself and cannot see it. The films require the audience to see around the protagonist's self-mythologizing to the damage beneath.

### Catholic Guilt and Redemption
Scorsese was raised devoutly Catholic in Little Italy. Sin, guilt, violence, and the desire for absolution run through all his work. Travis Bickle in Taxi Driver wants to purge New York of sin — and is himself the most sinful person in the film. Mean Streets opens with Charlie hearing a priest say his penance is done in the street. The Irishman ends in a nursing home, a desolate purgatory. Silence is his most direct engagement with faith and its cost.

## Essential Films

### Taxi Driver (1976) ★★★★★
Travis Bickle (Robert De Niro) is a Vietnam veteran and insomniac driving a cab through 1970s New York, which he perceives as a cesspool requiring violent cleansing. Paul Schrader's script is a first-person confessional — the film is Travis's projection of his own violence onto the city. Jodie Foster plays the 12-year-old prostitute he decides to save. The climax is operatically violent; Bernard Herrmann's final score (he died hours after completing it) is devastating. "You talkin' to me?" was improvised by De Niro.

### Raging Bull (1980) ★★★★★
The biography of middleweight boxing champion Jake LaMotta, shot in black and white by Michael Chapman. The boxing sequences are not realistic — they're impressionistic explosions of light, blood, and sweat designed to show the psychology of violence, not its mechanics. LaMotta (De Niro, who gained 60 pounds for the film's later scenes) destroys everything he touches: his marriage, his brother's loyalty, his career. One of the most formally perfect films ever made. Voted the greatest film of the 1980s in nearly every critical poll.

### GoodFellas (1990) ★★★★★
The defining American crime film. Henry Hill (Ray Liotta) narrates his life in the mob from childhood to witness protection with gleeful self-celebration. Nicholas Pileggi's source material was a journalistic account; Scorsese turned it into a kinetic, 146-minute rush that makes crime look like the best time anyone ever had — and then systematically dismantles that glamour. Joe Pesci won the Oscar for Tommy DeVito, one of cinema's most terrifying psychopaths. "As far back as I can remember, I always wanted to be a gangster."

### Casino (1995) ★★★★
Three hours inside the Las Vegas mob operation of the 1970s and 80s. Robert De Niro, Joe Pesci, Sharon Stone (Oscar nominated). Casino is GoodFellas's more operatic, more tragic sibling — where GoodFellas is about the seductiveness of crime, Casino is about its inevitability of failure. The casino itself becomes a character. Sharon Stone's Ginger is one of the great self-destructive female characters in American film.

### The Departed (2006) ★★★★★
A Massachusetts State Police officer (Leonardo DiCaprio) goes undercover in the Irish mob; simultaneously a mob plant (Matt Damon) infiltrates the police. Both sides know there's a mole; neither knows who. William Monahan's script is ferociously intelligent and funny. Jack Nicholson plays Frank Costello as pure id. The ending is brutal and correct — the only way a film this morally compromised can end. Scorsese's Oscar for this film was long overdue.

### The Wolf of Wall Street (2013) ★★★★★
Three hours of Jordan Belfort (DiCaprio) describing his own crimes with unabashed relish. The longest film Scorsese ever made is also the most morally complicated — by refusing to judge Belfort, by making his life look like fun, the film implicates the audience in the fantasy it's selling. Margot Robbie's debut. Jonah Hill supporting. The quaalude scene is the funniest thing Scorsese has ever filmed. The ending — Belfort at a seminar, the crowd leaning toward him — is the most damning in American film.

### The Irishman (2019) ★★★★★
Scorsese's late masterpiece and perhaps his most personal film. Frank Sheeran (De Niro), aging and alone in a nursing home, confesses to killing Jimmy Hoffa (Al Pacino). Three and a half hours of friendship, loyalty, violence, and consequence. The digital de-aging technology drew mockery but the purpose is correct — we see the young man inside the old one. The final act, Frank's desolate old age, is the most quietly devastating ending of Scorsese's career. A film about what crime actually costs, finally.

### Killers of the Flower Moon (2023) ★★★★★
The most morally serious film of Scorsese's career. Based on David Grann's account of the 1920s Osage murders: the systematic killing of Osage Nation members after oil was discovered on their land. Leonardo DiCaprio plays Ernest Burkhart, a weak man who participates in the murders of his wife's family while convincing himself he loves her. The film is three and a half hours about complicity, love, and the structures of white supremacy. Lily Gladstone's performance is the finest in the film.
